The Heat Equation

Comparing Numerical Solutions To The Heat Equation

Main Idea

The aim of this project is to test three different numerical methods that can be used in order to solve the heat equation, and observe the efficiency and stability of each. These methods are the forward euler, backward euler, and crank-nicholson methods.

Problem Statement

The time step size for this problem will be k=0.01, and the mesh step size will be h=0.02. We will observe the equation after 1 second in time has passed. We begin with the initial boundary value problem of the heat equation written as ut(t, x) − uxx(t, x) = 0. Our problem specific case will be represented as u(0, x) = e^−100x^2, where u(−1, t) = u(1, t) = 0.

Forward Euler

Plotted below in the first graph are 100 realizations of the heat equation as it passes through time going from 0 seconds to 1 seconds with step size k=0.01. The second plot shows the equation when the time is exactly 1 second. We can see that this method is extremely unstable, as we are getting an answer just shy of 1e182, which is massive.

Backward Euler

Plotted below in the first graph are 100 realizations of the heat equation as it passes through time going from 0 seconds to 1 seconds with step size k=0.01. The second plot shows the equation when the time is exactly 1 second. We can see that this method is extremely stable, since we move from the our initial starting point (the blue curve in the first graph) that has a high temparature, down to a temperature of almost zero as time passes and our system cools off. This cooled off system, when time is equal to 1 second, can be seen in the second graph (also a blue curve).

Crank-Nicolson Method

Plotted below in the first graph are 100 realizations of the heat equation as it passes through time going from 0 seconds to 1 seconds with step size k=0.01. The second plot shows the equation when the time is exactly 1 second. Much like the Backward Euler method, the Crank-Nicholson method is also stable. We can see how the curves "cool off" and decrease in temperature (as show by going from the blue to orange to green to red curves and so on).

Concluding Statements & Analysis of Findings

The most important thing to understand about the results of the graphs above is that the backwards Euler Method as well as Crank Nicolson Method are unconditionally stable, which is why we are able to observe them converging to approximate some solution when k=0.01. The forwards Euler Method on the other hand, is only stable when (k/h^2) < 0.5. This only occurs around k = 0.0001, which would yield 0.25 < 0.5. Since k=0.01, our stability equation produces (0.01/0.02^2)=25 which is not less than 0.5.
